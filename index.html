Title : Data Analytics-Project Development-Assessing Healthcare Disparities in the United States

1. PROJECT DESCRIPTION

The project aims to utilize data analytics to assess healthcare disparities across different demographics in the United States. By analyzing healthcare data, we seek to identify areas of inequality and provide insights for policy-makers and healthcare professionals to address these disparities effectively.

2. DASHBOARD CREATION

Key findings will be exported for visualization within IBM Cognos. The dashboard will include key metrics on racial disparities in healthcare access

3. DASHBOARD ANALYSIS:

The data set provided is a comprehensive table that presents data on the type of ownership and size of hospitals in the United States, categorized by the number of beds, from 1980 to 2013. The table is divided into sections for All hospitals, Nonfederal, Federal, Nonfederal government, For-profit (investor-owned), Not-for-profit (voluntary), Community hospital, and Outpatient surgery. Each section has data on the number of hospitals and average length of stay.

Here are some key observations:

o All Hospitals: The number of all hospitals decreased from 38,892 in 1980 to 36,242 in 2013. This indicates a reduction in the total number of hospitals over this period. The average length of stay in these hospitals also decreased over this period, suggesting improvements in healthcare efficiency or changes in admission policies.

o Federal Hospitals: The number of federal hospitals decreased significantly from 2,044 in 1980 to 826 in 2013. This could be due to a shift in healthcare policy, with a move towards privatization or a reduction in the number of federally funded healthcare facilities.

o Nonfederal Hospitals: The number of nonfederal hospitals decreased from 36,848 in 1980 to 35,334 in 2013. This suggests a slight decrease in the number of nonfederal hospitals, which could be due to various factors such as changes in healthcare policy, funding, or demand for services.

o For-Profit Hospitals: The number of for-profit hospitals increased from 3,165 in 1980 to 5,500 in 2013. This indicates a growth in the for-profit healthcare sector, possibly due to increased privatization or market opportunities in the healthcare industry.

o Community Hospitals: The number of community hospitals decreased from 36,143 in 1980 to 34,078 in 2013. This suggests a slight decrease in the number of community hospitals, which could be due to factors such as changes in community healthcare needs, funding, or policy changes.

o Outpatient Surgeries: The number of outpatient surgeries increased significantly from 262,951 in 1980 to 9,00,689 in 2013. This could indicate a shift towards more outpatient care, possibly due to advancements in medical technology allowing for more procedures to be performed on an outpatient basis, or changes in healthcare policy promoting outpatient care.

• Admissions to state-local government hospitals decreased steadily from 1980 to 2019.

Average Length of Stay

Type of ownership and size of hospital:

The table shows admissions data for various hospital categories based on ownership (federal, non-federal [split between non-profit and for-profit], and state-local government) and bed count size (e.g., 6-24 beds, 25-49 beds, etc.) You can analyze how admissions trends vary across these categories over a ten-year period (1980-2019).

admissions trends between for-profit and non-profit hospitals look different:

• Admissions to non-profit hospitals decreased steadily from 1980 to 2010, then increased slightly in the last three years (2017-2019).

• Admissions to for-profit hospitals showed a steady increase from 1980 to 2010, with some fluctuation between 2010 and 2019.

• In 1980, admissions at non-profit hospitals were more than double those at for-profit hospitals (25,566 vs. 3,165).

• By 2010, the gap had narrowed considerably (25,532 vs. 4,325).

• The trend appears to have reversed somewhat by 2019, with admissions at non-profit hospitals again exceeding those at for-profit hospitals (24,728 vs. 5,309).

4. CONCLUSION

It presents data on the type of ownership and size of hospitals in the United States from 1980 to 2013, reveals significant shifts in the healthcare landscape over this period. The overall decrease in the number of hospitals, particularly federal and community hospitals, could reflect changes in healthcare policy and funding, as well as the impact of economic and demographic trends.

The rise in the number of for-profit hospitals and outpatient surgeries suggests a shift towards privatization and outpatient care, possibly driven by advancements in medical technology, changes in healthcare policy, and market opportunities in the healthcare industry.

However, these trends should be interpreted in the context of broader healthcare, economic, and policy changes during this period. The data provides valuable insights for healthcare policy, planning, and resource allocation, and underscores the importance of continual monitoring and analysis of healthcare trends to inform decision-making and ensure the provision of quality healthcare services.

It’s also important to note that the data is up until 2013, and the current statistics may be different. Therefore, for a more accurate and up-to-date understanding of the current state of hospitals in the United States, more recent data should be consulted. This analysis serves as a historical reference point for understanding the evolution of the U.S. healthcare system over several decades. It highlights the dynamic nature of the healthcare sector and the need for adaptability and responsiveness to changing circumstances and needs.
